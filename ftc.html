FTC 24226 Warrior Robotics

2023 – 2024

I led the software for FTC 24226 Warrior Robotics, a 20-member rookie FIRST Tech Challenge team in the 2023–2024 CENTERSTAGE season. In our first year we won 1st place at the Southern Alberta Last Chance Qualifier and took the Think Award for programming. I wrote the team’s autonomous and driver-control code in Java on the FTC SDK — the vision routines that run during autonomous, plus PID and odometry control for the drivetrain and the scoring mechanisms.

Autonomous & vision

Most of a match is decided in the 30-second autonomous period, so that’s where I spent the most time.

  • The robot starts by figuring out where the team prop is. An EasyOpenCV pipeline looks at two regions of the camera frame and compares their average color to the alliance color; if neither matches, the prop must be in the third (off-camera) spot. That tells us whether it’s Left, Center, or Right.
  • Driving is handled by Road Runner. Instead of timed “drive forward for two seconds” moves, paths are built as trajectory sequences — splines, turns, and waits — and followed with feedforward plus feedback, so the robot traces the same smooth curve every run.
  • To keep that accurate, three dead wheels (unpowered encoder wheels) track the robot’s position on the field, so it still knows where it is even when the drive wheels slip.
  • I wrote 15+ autonomous routines, one for each starting setup (red/blue alliance × front/back tile), and each one branches on the prop position it detected.
  • I sketched and tested the paths in MeepMeep, a Road Runner simulator, before running them on the real robot — a lot faster than burning field time on every tweak.

Subsystem control

The scoring mechanisms run closed-loop too, so they hit the same positions every time:

  • A mecanum drivetrain, shared between autonomous and the driver-controlled period.
  • Linear slides on a PID loop using the motor encoders, so they reach a target height and hold it under load. The driver gets preset heights off the same controller.
  • The intake, slide, and arm are tied together, so the driver can grab, lift, and drop a pixel with one button instead of juggling each one.

Results

  • 1st place — Southern Alberta Last Chance Qualifier (rookie season)
  • Think Award — recognizing the team’s engineering portfolio and programming
  • 15+ autonomous routines across all alliance / starting-position combinations
  • Software lead for a 20-member rookie team

What I did

As software lead I wrote most of the code and got the rest of the programming subteam comfortable working in it. I tuned the Road Runner drive and odometry from scratch with the built-in feedforward and PID tuners, wrote the OpenCV pipeline and adjusted the color thresholds and sample regions for the lighting at our field, and built all the autonomous routines on top of that.

The biggest thing I took away was to favor reliability over ambition. An autonomous that scores a little but works every single match beats a fancy one that fails half the time — and for a rookie team, having autos that just worked was a big part of how we ended up with the Think Award.
